## What changed

Two developments after January 2026 are documented in the sources, both centered on Trump-era handling of government records.

**1. March 2026 — disclosure of a Jan. 13, 2023 Jack Smith / FBI memo to Congress.**
The Justice Department, in document productions to the Republican-run House Judiciary Committee on March 13, 2026, included previously non-public materials summarizing Special Counsel Jack Smith's classified-documents investigation as of January 2023. On March 25, 2026, Rep. Jamie Raskin (ranking Democrat on House Judiciary) sent a letter to Attorney General Pam Bondi characterizing the memo as containing "damning evidence." Reported allegations from the memo:
- Trump retained at least one document accessible to "only 6" people in the U.S. government, including the president. Prosecutors wrote: "That document is one we will need" / "We must have those documents."
- Some retained classified materials "would be pertinent to certain business interests," which prosecutors assessed established "a motive for retaining them."
- Materials posed "an aggravated potential harm to national security."
- A classified map prosecutors believed Trump "may have shown to individuals on board" his private plane on a June 2022 flight to Bedminster, NJ; the memo states this was witnessed by Susie Wiles, then CEO of Trump's super PAC and now White House chief of staff. DOJ provided a map of the aircraft with all passenger names redacted.

Raskin also alleged DOJ's disclosure to Congress may have violated Judge Aileen Cannon's sealing order and may have included Rule 6(e) grand-jury material ("Contains 6(e) and Sealed Material").

This claim is **contested**. The DOJ called Raskin's letter a "cheap political stunt" with "baseless" accusations, said Smith's files "contain salacious and untrue claims," and asserted Cannon's order was not violated. White House spokespeople (Karoline Leavitt, Abigail Jackson) called the claims "untrue and salacious" and said Trump "did nothing wrong." A source familiar with the case told CNN the business-interest consideration was "speculative" and did not play a major role in the criminal case. Trump has consistently denied wrongdoing.

**2. April 3, 2026 — DOJ Office of Legal Counsel opinion on the Presidential Records Act.**
DOJ's OLC issued an opinion (dated Thursday, ~April 2, 2026) declaring the Presidential Records Act unconstitutional, stating "the President need not further comply with its dictates." Per the reporting, no prior president had taken the position that the law violates the Constitution's separation-of-powers provisions. Donald Sherman of Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW) called it "part of the President's ongoing and escalating assault on transparency and oversight." This effectively advises that Trump may retain government records when leaving office, contrary to the Watergate-era statute.
